\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Topic and Problem Statement}

The recently passed Regulation 2025/2818 laying down additional procedural rules on the enforcement of Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (hereinafter `Procedural Regulation')\autocite{ProceduralRegulation} seeks to further proceduralize cross-border enforcement of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)\autocite{GDPR}. 

The GDPR is the cornerstone of the European Union's (EU) so-called Digital Acquis.\autocite[223]{zanfirGDPREDPS} 
Entered into force in 2018, and replacing the Data Protection Directive of 1995, it has often been heralded as the most comprehensive package of data protection and privacy regulations.\autocite[498]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026} 

The GDPR aims to provide a consistent and harmonized framework to protect the fundamental right to data protection across the EU\autocite[recital 7]{GDPR}, as enshrined in the Charter of Fundamental Rights (CFR)\autocite[8(1)]{CFR} and in the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU)\autocite[16(1)]{TFEU}. 
As recognized by the Regulation itself, the need for predictable and effective protection of personal data has become incresingly acute, given the scale at which information is processed.\autocite[recital 6]{GDPR}

The GDPR relies on a system of decentralized enforcement.\autocite[recital 1]{ProceduralRegulation} 
Under the GDPR, Member States (MS) are to establish independent Data Protection Authorities (DPAs), who are competent to carry out enforcement on their own national territory.\autocite[51(1) and (2)]{GDPR}

The enforcement design of the GDPR in cross-border cases rests on a one-stop-shop (OSS) principle,\autocite[recital 127]{GDPR} through the cooperation and consistency mechanism.\autocite[chapter VII]{GDPR} 
In an enforcement procedure concerning data processing in multiple MS, the Supervisory Authority (SA) of the MS where the controller has its main establishment assumes the role of Lead Supervisory Authority (LSA),\autocite[56(1)]{GDPR} becoming the sole interlocutor of the accused.\autocite[63]{facebookBelgium} 
Other SAs with a vested interest in the case become Supervisory Authorities Concerned (SAC),\autocite[4(22)]{GDPR} and are meant to collaborate with the LSA in carrying out enforcement.\autocite[63]{facebookBelgium}

While the choice of legislative instrument and the language of the GDPR highlight the need for a strong framework for data protection, which has become inseparable from the exercise of other fundamental rights in democratic societies, the enforcement of the GDPR has encountered practical challenges, especially in the context of cross-border cases. 
The Procedural Regulation seeks to address these challenges by further defining the steps involved in cooperation between MS' authorities, introducing earlier consensus-finding and time limits for every new stage of the procedure.

In particular, the literature has identified serveral key issues related to the enforcement design of the GDPR. Chiefly, a lack of harmonization in the domain of administrative procedures has been highlited as a key issue. 

This thesis seeks to analyze the Procedural Regulation, and its potential impact on the enforcement of the GDPR in cross-border cases. 
It will do so in light of the general principle of good administration, with a view to investigate whether the new procedural rules will lead to a more effective and consistent enforcement of the GDPR, contributing to the protection of fundamental rights across the EU.

\subsection{Literature Review}
\subsubsection{The Enforcement Challenges of the GDPR}

Multiple scholars and stakeholders of data protection enforcement have signaled flaws with the enforcement architecture of the GDPR. 
Gentile and Lynskey identified four flaws: the discrepancy between Member States' administrative procedures, the outsized role of LSAs, procedural fairness flaws, and a divergence of enforcement priorities and strategies between Member States.\autocite[806]{gentileDeficientDesign2022} 
The authors' fundamental conclusion is that enforcement under the GDPR is not truly cooperative, and that data protection enforcement is still beholden to national logic.\autocite[830]{gentileDeficientDesign2022}

In the same vein, a 2022 letter of the EDPB detailed similar concerns. 
The EDPB proposed further harmonization in the domain of administrative procedural law, highlighting concerns with the diverse interpretations of Member States' legal systems of key concepts relating to complaint handling.\autocite{EDPBLetterEUComm} 
The EDPB proposed the institution of additional time limits to ensure complaints are dealt with within a reasonable time.\autocite[s 2]{EDPBLetterEUComm} 
Similar concerns on the lack of harmonization and the OSS mechanism of the GDPR have been echoed by panels of experts invited by the European Data Protection Supervisor (EDPS),\autocite[20-25]{europeandataprotectionsupervisorEffectiveEnforcementDigital} as well as the European Commission in its second report on the application of the GDPR.\autocite{europeancommissionSecondReportApplication}

It must be pointed out that the enforcement design of the GDPR was born out of a compromise to ensure proximity to the complainants, with the SAC being the point of reference for data subjects.\autocite[812]{gentileDeficientDesign2022} 
MS in the Council rejected the idea of reducing the role of SACs, out of a need to ensure the protection of their own citizens especially in cases where processing activities would covered the entirety of the EU.\autocite[2]{councildebateSAC} 

In 2023, Mustert carried out an analysis of the GDPR's enforcement system against the constitutional principles of EU law.\autocite[12]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR} 
The author concluded that further harmonization in secondary law must be brought forth to give weight to those constitutional principles, to smooth out the differences in Member States' procedural laws and by rendering the cooperation procedure more efficient.\autocite[324-325]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR}

\subsubsection{The Procedural Regulation}

Against this backdrop, in July 2023 the Commission presented a proposal for a Procedural Regulation.\autocite{ProposalProcReg} 
The proposal was based on three issues: complaints, the procedural rights of parties under investigation, and the cooperation and dispute resolution mechanism.\autocite[s 1]{ProposalProcReg}

The Procedural Regulation was adopted in November 2025. 
In its final version, the Procedural Regulation harmonizes aspects relating to the admissibility\autocite[4]{ProceduralRegulation} and early resolution of complaints,\autocite[5]{ProceduralRegulation} the further proceduralization of administrative cooperation under Article 60 GDPR (including the institution of time limits),\autocite[ch III]{ProceduralRegulation} and the procedural rights of 'parties under investigation' and of complainants.\autocite[art 2 and s 5]{ProceduralRegulation}

As Mustert recently noted, the Procedural Regulation only applies to infringements with a cross-border element.\autocite[499]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026} 
The Procedural Regulation does little to harmonize national administrative procedure, instead opting to add more rigid procedures when investigating cross-border infringements.\autocite[499]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026} 
Mustert identifies that little is done to further the GDPR's role as a remedial tool for individuals, noting that complainants still suffer from a weak procedural position.\autocite[510]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026} 
The Regulation presents complaints as 'an essential source of information for detecting infringements of data protection rules',\autocite[510]{ProceduralRegulation} instead of viewing them as a mechanism to safeguard individual rights.\autocite[501]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026}

While the Regulation seems to leave unanswered a lot of the concerns with the ambiguity and inefficiency created by the clash of different MS' administrative procedures, it creates a rigid structure for cross-border investigations to follow.\autocite[506]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026} 
Mustert highlights this as a potential issue, since reduced flexibility in the absence of comprehensive administrative reform risks weakening the practical impact of the reform.\autocite[506-507]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026} 
This concern can be seen in conjunction with a wider consensus in the literature that DPAs' workload is often not sustainable due to a lack of resources.\autocites[5-8]{eufraGDPRPracticeExperiences2024}[246-247]{mustertFutureEffectiveEnforcementEDPS}{apGeenVerhogingBudget2021}

\subsubsection{Peer-pressuring the LSA into action?}

Notwithstanding the lack of comprehensive procedural reform, the regulation seems to act especially on one of the points raised by the scholarship and the institutional players: the role of LSAs in cross-border proceedings. 

The concern with the role of the LSA has been partially dealt with by the CJEU in Facebook Belgium.\autocite{facebookBelgium} 
The Court explained that while the LSA is the chief actor in a cross-border case, all SAs are collectively responsible for the protection of the rights afforded by the GDPR.\autocite[63-68]{facebookBelgium}

This framing may be viewed as a form of 'peer pressure', where enforcement is pushed forward by allowing horizontal cooperation.\autocite[466]{hofmannmustertScholtenHandbook2023} 
The architecture of the GDPR, supplemented by the Procedural Regulation, may be seen as a network of horizontal collaboration (between the DPAs) with a vertical element of norm-setting (in the form of the EDPB).\autocite[428]{mastenbroekFillingGapEuropean2018}

Having established the Procedural Regulation's weak expansion of individual rights, it is nonetheless useful to evaluate whether the rigid proceduralization of cross-border enforcement will be beneficial for Europeans' right to data protection. 
An important constitutional framework to evaluate the effectiveness of the reformed enforcement mechanism is the principle of good administration, which underpins administrative practice within the scope of Union law.\autocite[24]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR}

\subsubsection{The General Principle of Good Administration}

The principle of good administration exists in two forms within the Union legal order: as a right under Article 41 CFR,\autocite[41]{CFR} and as a general principle of law, developed by the CJEU's jurisprudence.\autocites[49]{HNvMinisterJustice}[69]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}[34]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR}

The concept of good administration encompasses many procedural requirements and rights.\autocite[74]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015} 
The CJEU provided a summary of these in the Tillack case, linking the concept of good administration to the rights enumerated in 41 CFR: `[…] the right to have affairs handled impartially, fairly and within a reasonable time, the right to be heard, the right to have access to files, or the obligation to give reasons for decisions […]'.\autocite[127]{Tillack}

The principle of good administration presents two facets: an objective and a subjective rationale.\autocite[111-112]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015} 
The objective rationale ensures the efficiency of the administration, while the subjective rationale is aimed at providing procedural protections for individuals.\autocite[111-112]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}

When Member States act within the scope of Union law, they must observe its fundamental principles.\autocite[21]{klagarenVHans} 
General principles of Union law have a primary law status,\autocite[1629]{lenaertsConstitutionalAllocationPowers2010} as does the CFR.\autocite[6(1)]{TEU} 
Therefore, when DPAs enforce the GDPR, their actions fall under the scope of Union law and are subject to the principle of good administration.

Since the Procedural Regulation mainly addresses procedural aspects relating to the cooperation mechanism, there is little to examine with regards to individual subjective rights. 
The objective element of the principle of good administration, however, seems to match the rationale of the Procedural Regulation: the imposition of time limits in the cooperation mechanism is aimed at bolstering efficiency,\autocite[recital 17]{ProceduralRegulation} while earlier consensus building could oblige the LSA to look at all possible legal facts before starting a case.\autocite[recitals 36-38]{ProceduralRegulation}

\subsection{Research Gap}
The Procedural Regulation has been adopted on 26 November 2025. 
It will enter into force on 2 April 2027. 
Mustert and Hofmann provided early commentary on the Commission Proposal in 2024 while it was in the trilogue stage,\autocite{hofmannFutureGDPREnforcement2024} and Mustert provided an early comment on the Regulation after its passing.\autocite{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026} 
However, no structured assessments of the reformed cooperation procedure against EU primary law have been conducted. 
This thesis will attempt to conduct such an assessment, following Mustert's example,\autocite{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR} albeit at smaller scale.

As seen in early commentary, the Procedural Regulation does not seek to harmonize national administrative procedure, but instead acts to require consensus at an earlier stage of the cooperation procedure, in the hopes that enforcement will be more consistent and participated.

    \item To what extent does Regulation 2025/2518 curb LSAs' discretionary powers in light of the duties of care and to give reasons under the general principle of good administration in the context of composite cross-border enforcement of the GDPR?
    \begin{itemize}
        \item What do the duty of care and duty to give reasons require of DPAs in the context of cross-border GDPR enforcement?
        \item What changes does the Procedural Regulation bring to the GDPR's cooperation and consistency mechanism?
        \item Are earlier consensus finding and new procedural deadlines under Regulation 2025/2518 adequate to fulfill the duty of care of DPAs?
        \item Will earlier consensus finding and new procedural deadlines contribute to respecting the duty to give reasons in composite administrative proceedings involving the EDPB?
        \item What consequences stem from a failure to observe the principle of good administration for the protection of fundamental rights in the context of cross-border enforcement of the GDPR?
    \end{itemize}
