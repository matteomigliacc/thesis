\section{The Duty of Care and the Procedural Regulation}

This chapter will provide a critical analysis of the impact of the Procedural Regulation on the respect of duty of care of \glspl{dpa} in the integrated enforcement system of the \gls{gdpr}.
As laid out in \hyperref[sec:DoC]{section 2.3.1}, the duty of care requires, (i) at all stages of the administrative procedure, (ii) an impartial and careful establishment of the facts and legal issues, with (iii) the most complete and reliable information, through (iv) an investigation that may go beyond what the parties have provided.
The chapter will examine how the deficiencies of the pre-reform system have impacted the respect of the duty of care, and whether the Procedural Regulation is suited to address those shortcomings.

\subsection{The Respect of the Duty of Care in the Pre-Reform System}

The cases outlined in section 3.2 show how the pre-reform mechanism of cooperation under the \gls{gdpr} has in practice failed to ensure the respect of the duty of care of \glspl{dpa}.
A reduced scope of investigation constrains the ability of \glspl{sac} to raise `relevant and reasoned objections', where the \gls{sac} must substantiate how their objection would lead to a different conclusion for an infringement finding, and provide reasoning on the legal and factual issues they contest.\autocite[paras 12--13 and 16--17]{edpbGuidelines092020}
Even when the \glspl{sac} were able to meet the threshold required, the \gls{edpb} found itself constrained in its ability to expand the scope of the violations, with its only option being ordering a new investigation by the \gls{lsa}.\autocite[25]{klozaIfItAint2026}

In particular, taking the elements of the duty of care into account, the pre-reform system failed in those cases to ensure that the investigation included the most complete and reliable information (iii) at all stages of the procedure (i).
When a procedural step to expand the scope of the investigation was triggered at the end, the \gls{edpb} was unable to establish an expansion of the facts and legal issues involved in the decision (ii), as no information had been gathered on those facts that could prove or disprove further alleged violations.

\subsection{Early Resolution and the Duty of Care}

The early resolution of complaints, discussed in section 3.3.3, seems particularly problematic from the perspective of the duty of care.
While one of the stated objectives of the Procedural Regulation is to make the enforcement of the \gls{gdpr} more efficient, and while timeliness is also an aspect of the right to good administration,\autocite[41(1)]{CFR} early resolution may function as a tool to avoid investigating infringements of data subject rights.

Early resolution procedures do not require \glspl{sa} to follow the new procedural steps,\autocite[5(6)]{ProceduralRegulation} nor can they be challenged by a relevant and reasoned objection,\autocite[23(1)(c)]{ProceduralRegulation} severely limiting the ability of \glspl{sac} to pressure the \gls{lsa} to fully investigate the complaint.
Furthermore, early resolution can be applied by the receiving \gls{sa} without the involvement of the other \glspl{sa}, completely bypassing the cooperation mechanism.\autocite[5(1)(a)]{ProceduralRegulation}

The complainant can challenge the decision to resolve the complaint, but it is unclear why a complainant should take it upon themselves to pressure a \gls{dpa} to fully investigate a complaint \emph{ex post},\autocite[III]{mustertTooComplexWork2026} when they have already notified the authority of a violation of the law.
As Mustert notes, clear guidance will be necessary to ensure that early resolution is not used to legitimize a failure to investigate complaints, which would be difficult to reconcile with the duty of administrations to investigate matters to their fullest (iii).\autocite[501]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026}
Early resolution may lead to complainants having a harder time using the \gls{gdpr} as a remedial tool, leading \glspl{sa} to prioritize complaints that form part of larger systemic violations or fit better into their national prioritization strategies.\autocites[501]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026}[III]{mustertTooComplexWork2026}

By avoiding the new and old procedural steps for cooperation, the early resolution mechanism may lead to situations where \glspl{dpa} are not required to investigate complaints to their fullest (iii), removing the peer pressure avenues that could steer them into effectively investigating complaints,\autocite[466]{mastenbroekFillingGapEuropean2018} and potentially leading to a failure to respect the duty of care.

\subsection{Simple Procedure and the Duty of Care}

The simple procedure, as outlined in section 3.4.3, largely resembles the pre-reform cooperation mechanism, excluding most of the new procedural steps and time limits.\autocite[6(1)]{ProceduralRegulation}
Similarly to early resolution, precise guidance will be necessary to ensure that the simple procedure is not used to avoid the new procedural steps, so that the new ways enforcement can be pushed forward are not bypassed. 
In this regard, it should be noted that the simple procedure can be turned into a complex one simply at the request of the \glspl{sac}.\autocite[6(3)]{ProceduralRegulation}

The simple cooperation procedure may still benefit from the more precise indication of what constitutes `all relevant information' to be exchanged between the \glspl{sa},\autocite[9]{ProceduralRegulation} as well as the creation of a new cooperation file,\autocite[26]{ProceduralRegulation} which may help the \glspl{sac} to develop a more complete understanding of the case and therefore be able to participate more meaningfully in the cooperation procedure.
The new requirements could therefore help increase the amount of information (iii) available at all stages of the procedure (i), helping the \glspl{sac} to participate meaningfully in the determination of the facts and legal issues (ii) of the case, albeit with less procedural pressure than in the complex procedure.

As an exception to the new rules on relevant and reasoned objections, the \glspl{sac} are not prohibited from raising objections on the scope of the investigations when objecting to the draft decision.
However, this may reproduce the same issues generated by the pre-reform cooperation mechanism, where the scope of the investigation was contested at the end, and the \gls{edpb} was not able to reach a decision on the objections due to a lack of information and evidence.

\subsection{Early Consensus-Finding Under the Complex Procedure}

As noted in the cases examined in section 3.2, some of the failures to enforce the \gls{gdpr} to its full extent were due to a lack of information and evidence, and a mismatch of scope between the investigation by the \gls{lsa} and the objections raised by the \glspl{sac}.

In this vein, the requirement for early consensus through the summary of key issues and the possibility of referral to the Board is welcome.\autocite[44]{edpbJointOpinion012023}
While consensus does not equate perfect enforcement, as there is nothing preventing the \glspl{sa} from all agreeing on a narrow scope of investigation, an objection by a \gls{sac} will trigger an obligation for the \gls{lsa} to refer the matter to the \gls{edpb} for a binding decision in case it is not followed.\autocite[11(6)]{ProceduralRegulation}
Unfortunately, the Procedural Regulation makes dispute resolution available only in complaint-based investigations, and not in own-volition investigations.\autocite[11(6)]{ProceduralRegulation}

Another positive change brought by the Procedural Regulation is found in the Board's new ability to request information from the \glspl{sa} when dispute resolution is engaged.
However, the Procedural Regulation does not yet empower the \gls{edpb} to conduct its own investigation, thus being still dependent on the information provided by the \glspl{sa} to reach a decision on the objections raised.

Dispute resolution at the scope-setting stage may therefore have positive effects on the respect of the duty of care in complaint-based investigations. 
Specifically, it may lead the integrated enforcement system of the \gls{gdpr} to investigate matters to their fullest (iii) from the earlier stages of the procedure (i), and beyond what the parties have provided (iv), offering a forum to test the connection between the logic and the facts of the case at an earlier stage of the procedure (ii).

However, the architecture of the dispute resolution mechanism at the scope-setting stage may incentivize the opening of own-volition investigations parallel to complaints, to avoid immediate referral to the \gls{edpb}.\autocite[505]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026}
\glspl{sac} who may disagree with the scope of an own-volition investigation will have to resort to the same tools present in the old system, namely mutual assistance and joint operations, to try to reach consensus with the \gls{lsa}.\autocite[505]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026}

\subsection{Are Deadlines the Panacea for the Cooperation Mechanism?}

The Procedural Regulation makes extensive use of deadlines for the various procedural steps it introduces. While the use of deadlines could be beneficial for the efficiency of the enforcement system, there are questions as to whether they are the right solution for the structural design issues of the cooperation mechanism.

Hofmann and Mustert found that \glspl{dpa} have so far found the formal tools provided by the \gls{gdpr} a burden on their limited resources, and instead often opt for informal cooperation through the \gls{imi} to better manage their workload.\autocite[466--467]{hofmannmustertScholtenHandbook2023}
The use of the informal procedure through the \gls{imi} made information exchange escape the formal tools the \gls{gdpr} provided for, since informal requests do not entail legal deadlines, failing to create the necessary peer pressure.\autocite[466--467]{hofmannmustertScholtenHandbook2023}
The Procedural Regulation puts even more emphasis on the use of mutual assistance and joint operations as a way to reach consensus at the start of the investigation, and throughout the procedure.

\glspl{sa} have been lamenting for several years their lack of resources, hindering their ability to effectively cooperate with each other, and fulfill their tasks under the law.\autocites[30]{APjaarverslag2025}[9]{APjaarverslag2024}[11--12]{APjaarverslag2020}
That lack of resources, as well as an increasing workload due to an increase in tasks under other regulations of the digital acquis\autocite{APtoezichtAI} -- which does not always come with an increase in funding or capacity\autocite{GroeiAPNoodzakelijk} -- may make it even more burdensome for \glspl{sa} to meet the deadlines laid down in the Procedural Regulation, as the \gls{edpb} and the \gls{edps} have also recognized.\autocite[45]{edpbJointOpinion012023} 

Experience with the \gls{gdpr} already shows that the manner in which \glspl{dpa} have coped with the inadequate resources -- both at home and in cooperation with other \glspl{dpa} -- has been to create prioritization strategies at national level.\autocites{BeleidsregelsAP}{PrioritizationCNPD}
Those strategies have reserved formal investigations to cases where significant and systemic violations of data protection law are present, or where the \gls{dpa} has enough resources to handle the complaints.\autocite[501]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026}
Diverging national strategies and priorities have already been identified as a source of deficient enforcement of the \gls{gdpr}, and the Procedural Regulation seems to provide more tools for \glspl{dpa} to demote complaints to less formal procedures, such as early resolution or the simple procedure.

Taking into account the fact that the Procedural Regulation only applies to cross-border enforcement, the complex system of procedural steps and deadlines may exacerbate the need of \glspl{dpa} to prioritize certain cases over others.
It was already evident that while \glspl{dpa} are national administrative agents, they must also allocate part of their resources and capacity to enforcement in other \glspl{ms}, as required by their duties of cooperation.\autocites[343]{hijmansEUConsitutionalGuardian2016}[112]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR}

An expansion of deadlines for cross-border complaints risks taking away even more resources from national enforcement, with \glspl{dpa} finding themselves obliged to prioritize enforcement outside their borders over the protection of fundamental rights within their own territory.\autocite[112]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR}

\subsection{The Enforceability of Procedural Steps and Time Limits}

Another important way in which the Procedural Regulation may not deliver on its promise to proceduralize enforcement, and thus, creating more pressure, is the question on the enforceability of the new procedural steps and time limits.

The Procedural Regulation does not provide for any sanctioning mechanism in case of non-compliance with the procedural steps and time limits.
On the contrary, Article 14 expressly states that non-compliance with procedural deadlines shall not affect the legality of the procedure or of the validity of the decision resulting from it.
Article 15 explicitly allows the judiciary to take into account a failure to comply with the time limits provided for the presentation of draft decisions and the adoption of a final decision.

The lack of consequences for non-compliance with the procedural steps of the enforcement procedure was an issue already raised twice by the \gls{edpb} and \gls{edps}.\autocites[para 50, see footnote 32]{edpbJointOpinion012023}[2.1]{EDPBLetterEUComm}
Mustert has also noted a risk for the Procedural Regulation to function as a mere internal organizational benchmark rather than as an enforceable procedural guarantee.\autocite[507]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026}

\subsection{Interim Conclusion}

The discussions in this chapter have highlighted how the reform brought by the Procedural Regulation may improve the ability of the integrated enforcement system of the \gls{gdpr} to effectively comply with the duty of care.
In abstract, the proceduralization of the cooperation mechanism in the complex procedure is welcome. 
The new mechanism brings clarity on the types of information to be exchanged between the \glspl{sa}, the timings of those exchanges, it gives more opportunities to extend the scope of an investigation, and may help make more explicit the choices of the \gls{lsa} and the \glspl{sac} throughout the procedure with the creation of a cooperation file.
The Procedural Regulation finds a procedural remedy to the issues that arose during cross-border enforcement.

Unfortunately, the GDPR's deficiencies in enforcement are not a mere matter of procedural nature, but exist in a more complex context of diverging national strategies, inadequate funding and resources, and an ever-increasing workload for \glspl{dpa}, that the Procedural Regulation is set to increase.

The use of deadlines and the introduction of new procedural steps may exacerbate the need for \glspl{dpa} to prioritize certain cases over others, and to opt for less formal procedures, such as early resolution or the simple procedure, which may not be subject to the new procedural steps and deadlines.
Without adequate funding, the Procedural Regulation may end up being used as a tool to investigate less, and to resolve more complaints without a full investigation.
Peer pressure will only be exerted by fellow \glspl{sa} that suffer from chronic underfunding and a lack of resources.
The effectiveness of this mechanism therefore will depend on the willingness of \glspl{ms} to fund their national \gls{dpa} adequately.\autocite[52(4)]{GDPR}
