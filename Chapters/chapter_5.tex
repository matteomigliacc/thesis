\section{The Duty to Give Reasons and the Procedural Regulation}

As discussed in section 2.3.2, administrations have a duty to provide the parties affected by their decisions a `clear and relevant' explanation of the points of law and of fact that a decision is based upon, to enable the parties affected by that decision to understand their legal standing.
The performance of this duty according to Craig is crucial for three reasons: it allows the parties to understand the reasons for a decision affecting them, it allows the judiciary to review the legality of the decision, and it serves as an internal check on the administration, as it requires the administration to lay out its reasoning based on the facts and the law.

The composite administrative enforcement model of the \gls{gdpr} as amended by the Procedural Regulation, presents many intricacies related to the exercise of this duty.
The enforcement model of the \gls{gdpr} presents a complex network of horizontal, vertical and sometimes even diagonal relationships between the \gls{lsa}, the \glspl{sac}, and the \gls{edpb}, which are all meant to collaborate in the enforcement of the \gls{gdpr} and ultimately contribute to the assessments included in the final decision of the \gls{lsa}.

This chapter will explore the duty of administrations to give reasons in the context of the new cooperation mechanism introduced by the Procedural Regulation, examining the three dimensions of this duty as observed by Craig.

\subsection{The Duty to Give Reasons vis-à-vis the Parties}

At the most basic level, in complaint-based investigations, the \gls{lsa} will be the actor adopting a decision to accept a complaint, whereas the \gls{sa} receiving the complaint will be the actor adopting a decision to reject it, in whole or in part.
This system is meant to ensure that, in case a complainant would like to challenge the rejection of their complaint, they would be able to do so with the courts of the \gls{sa} that is proximate to them.
Therefore, the \gls{lsa} has a duty to give reasons with what concerns the acceptance of a complaint, whereas the receiving \gls{sa} has a duty to give reasons for the dismissal of a complaint.
On the contrary, from the point of view of the party against investigation, the \gls{lsa} will, of course, address any decision to them.
Those parties will always have the right to challenge the decision of the \gls{lsa} before the courts.

The Procedural Regulation introduces a new procedural step in the cooperation mechanism, which requires the \gls{lsa} to elaborate `preliminary findings'.
Preliminary findings must be provided to the parties under investigation by the \gls{lsa}, and to the complainant by the receiving \gls{sa}.
The prelimiary findings must `set out clearly all the facts, the list of evidence relied upon and the entire legal assessment raised against the parties under investigation'.
The Procedural regulation sets out a more detailed procedure to be followed when addressing the parties under investigation at this stage, leaving communivation with the complainant subject to the national laws of \glspl{ms}.
The Procedural Regulation also explicitly states that parties under investigation are not in the same position as the complainant and puts the emphasis on their right to defense.\autocite[recital 50]{ProceduralRegulation}
Importantly, the preliminary findings are binding on the \gls{lsa}, who may, as a rule, not deviate from the issues that it has raised with the parties under investigation, who have a right to be heard on those issues.
This requirement does not seem to apply to the complainant with the same strength.

Under the procedural regulation, when a \gls{lsa} intends to fully or partially rejecting a complaint, it must now state the reasons why it considers to do so to the complainant, through the receiving \gls{sa}.
This step must be taken before the \gls{lsa} prepares a draft decision.
The complainant must be then given the opportunity to make their views heard on those reasons.

\subsection{The Duty to Give Reasons vis-à-vis other SAs}

The cooperation mechanism of the \gls{gdpr} is based on the sharing of information and the exchange of views, legal findings and assessments between the \glspl{sa}.
The duty to give reasons is mostly reserved for those decisions that have an impact on the parties, and does not generally include internal communication between the \glspl{sa}.
The Procedural Regulation however introduces many more opportunities for the \glspl{sa} to exchange their views and legal assessments, with a view of peer pressuring each other to reach consensus at the start, during, and at the final stage of the enforcement procedure.
This exchange, paired with the possibility for formal objections as procedural steps, recalls the third function of reason-giving as explained by Craig.

On top of the already existing relevant and \emph{reasoned} objections to draft decisions, the Procedural Regulation provides \glspl{sac} tools to challenge the assessments of the \gls{lsa} at the earliest stages of the investigation, as seen in the previous sections. 
The \gls{lsa} is required to submit its summary of key issues, highlighting the legal and factual issues that it considers relevant.
The \glspl{sac} may challenge those assessments, and in doing so, excercise their ability to give reasons based on the information gathered by the \gls{lsa} and their own legal assessment of the case.
Similarly, the \glspl{sac} may challenge the extension of the time limits for delivering a draft decision, or comment on the preliminary findings of the \gls{lsa} to be submitted to the parties under investigation.
Many of the procedural steps inroduced by the Procedural Regulation heavily rely on the \glspl{sa}' ability to give reasons for their views and their legal assessments, and their ability to defend them before the \gls{edpb} in case of a dispute.

\subsection{The Duty to Give Reasons vis-à-vis the Judiciary and the EDPB}

This section will deal with the second function of reasoned-decisions, relating to the ability of the judiciary to follow the reasoning of the administration and review the legality of its decisions.
With its composite decision-making model, the \gls{gdpr} presents many challenges from the point of view of judicial review.
These stem from the fact that the final act adopted by the \gls{lsa} is the result of the input of many different actors in different \glspl{ms} -- and therefore, under different jurisdictions -- and the \gls{edpb}.

\subsubsection{The Role of the EDPB}
The role of the \gls{edpb} in the consistency procedure is crucial.
The \gls{edpb} acts both as a norm-setting body, as it interprets the \gls{gdpr} and sets out guidelines for its application, but acts also as a dispute resolution body and as a decision-maker in individual cases.
The Procedural Regulation expands the scope of action of the \gls{edpb} as a settler of disputes, as examined in section 3.4.8.
The \gls{edpb} must now settle disputes on the scope of the investigation, as well as ruling on any potential activations of the urgency procedure after the \gls{lsa} fails to deliver a draft decision within the time limit.

\subsubsection{The Role of the Judiciary in Examining the Legality of the Decisions}
The mechanism of enforcement of the \gls{gdpr} creates grey areas from the standpoint of the revieability of the decisions taken within the cooperation mechanism.
The final decision of the \gls{lsa} may be the result of a complex process of objections, dispute settlements, input from the \gls{edpb} and the \glspl{sac}.
Here, the procedural rights of complainants and of the parties under investigation are not the same, as even the Procedural Regulation states.

The parties under investigation, other than having a right to be heard on the preliminary findings, will receive a final decision that will directly be addressed to them, from an authority in their same jurisdiction, that they will be able to challenge before the courts.
In the recent appeal to the \gls{cjeu} of WhatsApp Ireland v \gls{edpb} -- challenging the binding decision in the case at 4.1.2 -- it was also ruled that a decision of the \gls{edpb} is not merely a preparatory act of a national measure, but is open to an action for annulment before the \gls{cjeu} under Article 263 \gls{tfeu}.\autocite[73--76]{appealWhatsAppEDPB}
Parties under investigation, therefore, have the possibility to challenge both the final, national, administrative act in front of the courts of the \gls{ms}, and the decision of the \gls{edpb} directly to the \gls{cjeu}.

Complainants do not benefit from the same right.
As explored in section 5.1, the complainant may receive receive a decision from the \gls{lsa}, the \gls{sa} they filed a complaint with, or both, depending on the outcome of the case.
Manifestly, this architecture was designed to allow complainants to challenge the rejection of their complaint before the courts of the \gls{ms} that is proximate to them.
However, in case a complainant would like to both challenge the validity of the final decision adopted as well as the rejection of their complaint, they would have to do so in two different jurisdictions.
The nature of this integrated system of enforcement also creates situations where a decision made by an \gls{sa} in the course of the procedure may have significantly impacted a complainant in another \gls{ms}, raising a question on the reviewability, by the courts of the complainant's \gls{ms}, of a decision taken by an administrative authority of another \gls{ms}.
Mustert and Hofmann argue that the national court of where the action against the final decision is brought may carry out judicial review of the legality of acts outside its national jurisdiction, taking EU law as a parameter.\autocite[470]{hofmannmustertScholtenHandbook2023}

In this vein, the Procedural Regulation does mention the possibility for a challenge by the complainant or the parties under investigation of one of the procedural steps of the cooperation mechanism.
This challenge should preferably be made available only in conjucntion with an action against the final decision, unless the procedural step in question will irreversibly affect the rights of that party.
The concerns with this system of judicial review have been echoed by AG Bobek in \emph{Facebook Belgium}, where he argued that challenging the rejection of a complaint by an authority who was not the final decision-maker, makes that administrative act an `empty shell', a mere formality.\autocite[106]{fbBelgiumAgBobek}
