\section[The Cooperation and Consistency Mechanism Pre-- and Post--Procedural Regulation]{The Cooperation and Consistency Mechanism\\Pre-- and Post--Procedural Regulation}

The cooperation and consistency mechanism forms the core of the enforcement design of the \gls{gdpr} for cross-border processing, through a network of horizontal cooperation between \glspl{dpa} with a vertical decision-maker and norm setter in the \gls{edpb}.
Networks of horizontal cooperation like the \gls{gdpr}'s, as seen in section 1.1.3, may increase the quality and speed of enforcement through a mechanism of peer pressure.\autocites[466]{hofmannmustertScholtenHandbook2023}[428]{mastenbroekFillingGapEuropean2018}
This chapter will first explore the pre--reform enforcement mechanism, with special regard to the ways in which \glspl{sa} have been able to influence one another throughout the process.
It will map the ways in which the cooperation and consistency mechanism has failed to deliver effective avenues to steer enforcement into more effective outcomes, as detailed in the literature and individual enforcement cases.
Finally, the chapter will analyze the key changes to the flow of cooperation introduced by the Procedural Regulation, with a view of understanding whether the reform will bring more (effective) legal avenues to push enforcement forward through peer pressure.

\subsection{The Tasks and Powers of Supervisory Authorities in Cross-Border Cases}

Under the \gls{gdpr}, \glspl{ms} are required to establish independent\autocite[52]{GDPR} \glspl{sa}, entrusted with monitoring the application of the Regulation.\autocite[51]{GDPR} 
Importantly, the \gls{gdpr} requires \glspl{sa} to all contribute to the application of the Regulation throughout the Union.\autocite[51(2)]{GDPR} 
The \gls{sa} is competent for enforcing the Regulation within the territory of its \gls{ms},\autocite[51(1)]{GDPR} with well-defined tasks\autocite[57]{GDPR} and powers\autocite[58]{GDPR}.
Among the tasks of the \gls{sa} figure: the monitoring of the application of the \gls{gdpr};\autocite[57(1)(a)]{GDPR} the handling of complaints;\autocite[57(1)(f)]{GDPR} the cooperation with other \glspl{sa} to ensure the consistency of application of the \gls{gdpr};\autocite[57(1)(g)]{GDPR} and the handling of investigations, including with the input of other \glspl{sa}.\autocite[57(1)(h)]{GDPR}
The powers of the \gls{sa} include investigative, corrective, advisory powers, as well as the power to engage in legal proceedings.\autocite[58]{GDPR}

\subsubsection{Competence Allocation in Cross-Border Enforcement Cases}

In the case of cross-border processing, the \gls{lsa} becomes competent to supervise the processing activities of the controller or processor when its main or single establishment is in its territory.\autocite[56(1)]{GDPR}
The \gls{lsa} becomes the sole interlocutor of the controller or processor responsible for the cross-border processing at examination.\autocite[56(6)]{GDPR}
Cross-border processing refers to either the processing of personal data by a controller or processor with multiple establishments across the Union, or the processing of personal data by one entity that `affects or is likely to affect' data subjects in more than one \gls{ms}.\autocite[4(23)]{GDPR}

Other \glspl{sa} then may become `concerned'.\autocite[4(22)]{GDPR} 
An \gls{sac} is concerned when either: the processing in question is carried out by an establishment in its territory; the data subjects residing in the territory of its competence are affected by the processing; or when a complaint has been lodged with it.\autocite[4(22)(a)--(c)]{GDPR}

\subsection{The Pre-Reform Cooperation and Consistency Mechanism}

\subsubsection{The Cooperation Procedure}

Articles 60 and 61 \gls{gdpr} set out the cooperation procedure between the \gls{lsa} and the \glspl{sac} in cases involving cross-border processing. 
Fundamentally, the \gls{lsa} is required to cooperate with the \glspl{sac} to reach consensus, with the authorities exchanging `all relevant information with each other'.\autocite[60(1)]{GDPR}
This endeavour does not form a legal obligation, but rather an objective, and reflects the wishes of the legislator to bring about a system mandatory cooperation between the \glspl{sa}.\autocite[39--43]{edpbGuidelines022022}

While investigating potential infringements, \glspl{sa} can make reciprocal requests for mutual assistance.\autocite[60(2), 61(1)]{GDPR} 
Mutual assistance includes requests for information, investigations, and inspections.\autocite[61(1)]{GDPR}
\glspl{lsa} can also make use of joint operations, where the \gls{lsa} and \glspl{sac} can carry out joint investigations, inspections, or monitor the application of corrective measures.\autocite[60(2), 62]{GDPR}
The ability to exchange information closely relates to forming consensus on a case, and it is a necessary obligation to respect to enable all \glspl{sa} to form an opinion on the case and fulfill their roles under the law.\autocite[44--45]{edpbGuidelines022022}

These informational tools have encountered practical hurdles as evidenced by Hofmann and Mustert.\autocite[465--467]{hofmannmustertScholtenHandbook2023}
While peer pressure through information exchange duties may push enforcement forward, \glspl{dpa} have experienced the formal instruments offered by the pre-reform system as a burden on their limited resources.\autocite[466]{hofmannmustertScholtenHandbook2023}
Additionally, the \gls{gdpr} does not provide a clear definition of what `all relevant information' means, thus allowing the \glspl{sa} to determine for themselves what information to make available through contextual criteria.\autocite[466]{hofmannmustertScholtenHandbook2023}
Without information, however, \glspl{dpa} lack the knowledge necessary to be an active part of the decision-making process, thus limiting their ability to advocate for a certain enforcement outcome.\autocite[466--467]{hofmannmustertScholtenHandbook2023}

\paragraph{Relevant and Reasoned Objections}
A lack of sufficient information becomes especially problematic in the final stages of the cooperation procedure.
In fact, the \glspl{sac} have the opportunity to make their views known regarding the draft decision of the \gls{lsa} at the end of its investigation.\autocite[60(3)]{GDPR}
\glspl{sac} may do so by submitting a `relevant and reasoned objection' to the draft.\autocite[60(4)]{GDPR}
An objection is regarded as `relevant and reasoned' if `clearly demonstrates the significance of the risks posed by the draft decision' concerning the fundamental rights and freedoms of data subjects.\autocite[4(24)]{GDPR}
The \glspl{sac} have a high threshold to clear, with the objection presenting `clearly and precisely' the link between the consequences the draft decision will bring about, and the significance of the risk for data subjects' fundamental rights and freedoms.\autocites[18--19]{edpbGuidelines092020}[811]{gentileDeficientDesign2022}
General comments are not enough to meet this threshold,\autocite[417]{mustertEDPBsteppingup2021} thus demanding an in-depth engagement with each individual case on the part of the objecting \gls{sa}.\autocites[811]{gentileDeficientDesign2022}[466]{hofmannmustertScholtenHandbook2023}

An \gls{lsa} can follow the objections presented, and adjust the draft decision accordingly.\autocite[60(5)]{GDPR}
In reality, \glspl{sac} have struggled to meet the high threshold required.
In those cases, when the \gls{lsa} does not follow the objections, or it does not believe that they are relevant or reasoned, the matter must be referred to the consistency mechanism.\autocite[60(4)]{GDPR}

If the period of four weeks lapses, and no objections are raised, the \gls{lsa} can adopt the decision and the \glspl{sac} are deemed to have agreed to it.\autocite[60(6)]{GDPR} 
When a decision is adopted, or a complaint is dismissed or rejected, the \glspl{sac} who received the original complaint are tasked with notifying the complainants of the decision.\autocite[60(7)--(8)]{GDPR}
When a complaint is only followed in part, the \gls{lsa} must inform the complainant of the points raised where it chose to act, while the \gls{sac} must adopt a separate decision dismissing the rejected parts of the complaint.\autocite[60(9)]{GDPR}

\subsubsection{The Consistency Mechanism}

The consistency mechanism is an extension of the cooperation procedure, meant to bring a consistent enforcement of the \gls{gdpr} across the Union.\autocites[63]{GDPR}[68]{googlevCNIL}[52]{facebookBelgium}
It is laid out in Articles 63--67 \gls{gdpr}, and can be triggered in various ways in the context of cross-border enforcement.

Firstly, the \gls{edpb} can issue non-binding opinions on matters of general application on the request of an \gls{sa}, its own chair, or the Commission.\autocite[64(2)]{GDPR}
This forms a first tool at the disposal of a \gls{sac} in case an \gls{sa} refuses to comply with a request for mutual assistance or joint operations as discussed by AG Bobek in \emph{Facebook Belgium}.\autocites[point 120--121]{fbBelgiumAgBobek}[64(2)]{GDPR}
While an opinion is non-binding, a binding decision can be requested in the event that an opinion is not followed, creating an implicit --  albeit cumbersome to operationalize -- incentive for the authorities to follow that opinion.\autocites[points 120--122]{fbBelgiumAgBobek}[318]{matosbrandaoOnestopshopEuropeanData2023}

\paragraph{Binding Decisions of the EDPB}
In addition to issuing binding decisions on the failure to follow an \gls{edpb} opinion, the Board can issue binding decisions in a number of instances.\autocite[65(1)]{GDPR}
The \gls{edpb} can resolve disputes between \glspl{sa} when there are conflicting views as to which \gls{sac} is competent for the main establishment of a controller or processor.\autocite[65(1)(b)]{GDPR}
Most importantly, the \gls{edpb} functions as a dispute settler in the cooperation procedure, as mentioned in the previous section.
When a \gls{sac} raises a relevant and reasoned objection to a draft decision of the \gls{lsa}, and the \gls{lsa} does not follow the objection, the \gls{lsa} is obliged to refer the matter to the \gls{edpb}.\autocite[65(1)(a)]{GDPR}

It is important to note that the \gls{edpb} does not have investigative powers of its own, and functions as a supporter of the main enforcers of the \gls{gdpr}, which remain the national \glspl{dpa}.\autocite[491--492]{britobastosCentralisedGeneralData2023}
The \gls{edpb} is therefore bound by the information gathered by the national \glspl{sa},\autocite[468]{hofmannmustertScholtenHandbook2023} and can only rule on the matters that national authorities have asked it to rule upon.\autocite[492]{britobastosCentralisedGeneralData2023}

The limitations of the \gls{edpb}'s role have already emerged in practice where the Board was not able, in a number of cases, to side with the \glspl{sac} who successfully brought a reasoned and relevant objection.
Examples include the \emph{Twitter Ireland} case, where the Board recognized that while further infringements of the \gls{gdpr} might have been committed by Twitter, the limited scope of the investigation started by the Irish \gls{sa} meant that it did not have elements to rule upon such alleged infringements.\autocite[132]{edpbTwitter}
In a similar vein, the \gls{edpb} compelled the Irish \gls{sa} to initiate a new investigation in the case of \emph{Instagram Ireland}, because it found that too little evidence was available to rule on potential infingements of Article 9, which were excluded from the scope of the investigation.\autocite[200--203]{edpbInstagram}
\todo[inline]{add the other cases from the same year with the same outcome}
In \emph{Instagram}, the Board particularly emphasized that a reduction in the scope of the investigation constrains the capacity of the \glspl{sac} to effectively act and tackle the risks to data subjects, and impairs the \gls{edpb}'s capacity to ensure a consistent application of the \gls{gdpr}.\autocite[200]{edpbInstagram}

Conversely, in the case of \emph{WhatsApp Ireland}, the Board was able to accept the relevant and reasoned objection of the Italian \gls{sa}.\autocite[183--201]{edpbWhatsApp}
However, the Board was only able to expand the legal scope of the decision insofar as the investigated breaches of the \gls{gdpr} concerning informational duties by WhatsApp were systematic and grave enough to rise to a violation of the fundamental principle of data processing in Article 5(1)(a).\autocite[198--201]{edpbWhatsApp}
All three decisions highlight the importance of early scope setting and consensus, in order to effectively enable the \glspl{sa} to meaningfully participate in the enforcement procedure, as discussed in the previous section.\autocites[132--136]{edpbTwitter}[225]{edpbWhatsApp}[200-203]{edpbInstagram}[468]{hofmannmustertScholtenHandbook2023}

\paragraph{The Urgency Procedure}
Finally, another instrument of pressure at the disposal of \glspl{sac} is found in the urgency procedure, when an \gls{sa} believes that there is an urgent need to act to protect the fundamental rights and freedoms of data subjects.\autocite[63]{facebookBelgium}
The urgency procedure allows any \gls{sa} to adopt urgent provisional measures, with a maximum effect of three months, on the territory of their \gls{ms}.\autocite[66(1)]{GDPR}
\glspl{sac} must inform all the other \glspl{sa}, the \gls{edpb}, and the Commission of their urgent provisional measure, and may request an urgent opinion or binding decision from the \gls{edpb}, if it considers that final measures must be urgently adopted.\autocite[66(2)]{GDPR}
Alternatively, the \gls{edpb} can also issue urgent opinions or binding decisions on the request of an \gls{sa} even when it does not adopt measures, when the \gls{sa} believes there is an urgent need to act to protect the fundamental rights and freedoms of data subjects.\autocite[66(3)]{GDPR}

The urgency procedure can also be triggered following the failure of another \gls{sa} to comply with the cooperation procedure.
When an \gls{sa} fails to comply with a request for mutual assistance, the threshold for the urgency procedure is considered to be met, allowing an \gls{sa} to adopt provisional measures, as well as requesting an urgent binding decision from the \gls{edpb}.\autocite[61(8)]{GDPR}
Additionally, when an \gls{lsa} does not invite a \gls{sac} to participate in joint operations, or ignores a request of a \gls{sac} to participate within one month, the \gls{sac} may also adopt an urgent provisional measure, requiring an urgent binding decision of the \gls{edpb}.\autocite[62(7)]{GDPR}

\subsection{The Post-Reform Cooperation and Consistency Mechanism}

The previous section explored the failures of the pre-reform cooperation and consistency mechanism, and how the formal avenues at the disposal of \glspl{sa} meant to pressure the \gls{lsa} into action have been ineffective in practice. 
This section will explore the changes to the cooperation and consistency mechanism introduced by the Procedural Regulation, with a focus on new avenues for peer pressure through the use of formal cooperation tools.
Special regard will be given to the tools concerning early consensus-building, information sharing, and the use of the dispute settlement mechanism.

For the purposes of this section, a `receiving \gls{sa}' refers to the \gls{sa} receiving the initial complaint, before the allocation of competence is determined.
Additionally, the moment of confirmation of the competence of the \gls{lsa} is referred to as $T_0$.

\subsubsection{``All Relevant Information'' and the Cooperation File}

The Procedural Regulation outlines a list of information that the \glspl{sa} must exchange during the enforcement procedure, with an obligation for the \gls{lsa} to share that information within one week of its availability.\autocite[9]{ProceduralRegulation}
The information will be added to a new cooperation file, accessible to the \glspl{sac} and to the \gls{edpb} when activating the consistency mechanism.\autocite[26(2)]{ProceduralRegulation}
The information will include, \emph{inter alia}, the information on the opening of an investigation, on its scope, as well as information gathered through the use of the \gls{gdpr}'s investigative powers.\autocites[26]{ProceduralRegulation}[504]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026}

With information exchange being a key aspect of consensus building, and given the ambiguities surrounding the meaning of ``all relevant information'' in the pre-reform system, the new provisions on information exchange may increase the amount of information available to \glspl{sac} and to the \gls{edpb} to form their views on a case.
As noted by Mustert, however, a rigid list of requirements may also lead to reduced flexibility. 
As an example, the list of information to be shared only includes information obtained through Article 58(1) \gls{gdpr}, potentially excluding information obtained on the basis of investigative powers laid down in national law.\autocite[504]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026}

\subsubsection{The Proceduralization of Competence Attribution}

A receiving \gls{sa} must now formulate a preliminary conclusion on the cross-border nature of the processing and the \gls{sa} it considers competent as \gls{lsa}\autocite[4(4)]{ProceduralRegulation}
The receiving \gls{sa} must, within six weeks, transmit the complaint to the \gls{lsa}\autocite[4(5)]{ProceduralRegulation}, who will have six weeks to confirm its competence or contest it by referring the matter to the \gls{edpb}.\autocite[4(6)]{ProceduralRegulation}
If the \gls{lsa} is inactive, the receiving \gls{sa} refers the matter to the \gls{edpb} for dispute resolution.\autocite[4(6)]{ProceduralRegulation}

The institution of time limits and the possibility for a \gls{sac} to refer the matter to the \gls{edpb} in case of inactivity provides a new tool for pushing enforcement forward, and may avoid potential administrative inertia surrounding the earliest stage of the procedure.\autocite[503]{mustertProceduralisingEnforcement2026}

\subsubsection{Three New Tracks for Cross-Border Enforcement Cases}

The Procedural Regulation provides for three tracks for the resolution of complaints relating to cross-border processing: early resolution, simple cooperation, and complex cooperation.

\paragraph{Early Resolution} 
Through early resolution, a complaint is dismissed when the alleged infringement has been brought to an end, making the complaint `devoid of purpose'.\autocite[5(2)]{ProceduralRegulation}
The procedure can be applied to cases that concern cross-border infringements relating to the rights of data subjects laid out in ch III of the \gls{gdpr}.\autocites[5(1)]{ProceduralRegulation}[ch III]{GDPR}
Early resolution can be triggered by the receiving \gls{sa} before the complaint is transmitted to the \gls{lsa}, or by the \gls{lsa} after receiving the complaint.\autocite[5(1)]{ProceduralRegulation}

The \gls{sa} dismissing the complaint must establish, based on evidence, that the infringement has been brought to an end.\autocite[5(2)]{ProceduralRegulation}
When a complaint is dismissed through early resolution, the dismissing \gls{sa} must inform the complainant of the end of the infringement, that it considers the complaint `devoid of purpose', of the consequences of early resolution, and of their right to object to early resolution within four weeks.\autocite[5(2)]{ProceduralRegulation}
When a complaint is dismissed by the receiving \gls{sa}, the receiving \gls{sa} establishes the resolution of the complaint within two weeks from the end of the objection period, and informs all parties.\autocite[5(3)]{ProceduralRegulation}
When a complaint is dismissed by the \gls{lsa}, the \gls{lsa} submits a draft decision to the \glspl{sac} within four weeks from the expiration of the objection period.\autocite[5(4)]{ProceduralRegulation}
That draft decision, however, will not be contestable through a relevant and reasoned objection, while it would be through the simple or complex procedure.\autocite[23(1)(c)]{ProceduralRegulation}
It is important to note that when early resolution is employed, the procedure does not follow most of the new procedural requirements provided for by the Procedural Regulation.\autocite[5(6)]{ProceduralRegulation}

The early resolution procedure therefore excludes many of the new avenues provided to \glspl{sa} to push enforcement forward, which will be detailed later.

\paragraph{Simple Cooperation} 
The Procedural Regulation creates a simple cooperation procedure, which largely resembles the previous, non-proceduralized cooperation procedure. 
A complaint can be handled through a simple cooperation procedure by the \gls{lsa} when, after having formed a preliminary view on the case, the \gls{lsa} believes that: 
\begin{enumerate*}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item there is no doubt regarding the scope of the investigation;
    \item and the case does not present legal or factual complexity that would require additional cooperation with the \glspl{sac}.
\end{enumerate*}\autocite[6(1)]{ProceduralRegulation}
According to the recitals, simple cooperation should not be employed in cases where there is a systemic risk or recurring problems in multiple \glspl{ms}, when the case intersects with other legal fields other than data protection, or when the case affects a large number of data subjects or is based on multiple complaints across the Union.\autocite[recital 26]{ProceduralRegulation}

The \gls{lsa} must inform the \glspl{sac} of its intention to handle the case through simple cooperation within six weeks from $T_0$.\autocite[6(2)]{ProceduralRegulation}
Any \gls{sac} may object to the application of the simple cooperation procedure within two weeks of the notification, and the procedure will continue according to the new complex procedure.\autocite[6(3)]{ProceduralRegulation}

Under the simple procedure, an \gls{lsa} must deliver a draft decision to the \glspl{sac} within twelve months of $T_0$.\autocite[12(6)]{ProceduralRegulation}
Most the new procedural requirements and time-limits established by the Procedural Regulation do not apply to the simple procedure.\autocite[6(1)]{ProceduralRegulation}
This entails that the new consensus-building tools will not, at least formally, be applicable to the simple procedure, although \glspl{sa} are still under the general obligation to reach consensus on the investigation.\autocites[8]{ProceduralRegulation}[60(1)]{GDPR}

\paragraph{Complex Cooperation}
The biggest changes introduced by the Procedural Regulation concern a new cooperation flow, referred to as `complex procedure'. 
It must be noted at the outset, that the Procedural Regulation specifically states that the new procedural steps and time limits do not influence the rights of the parties under investigation and complainants,\autocite[7]{ProceduralRegulation} nor that missing a time limit will influence the validity or legality of a decision.\autocite[14]{ProceduralRegulation}

At start of an investigation, after forming a preliminary view on a case, an \gls{lsa} must now draft a summary of key issues and present it to the \glspl{sac} within three months from $T_0$.\autocite[10(1), (3)]{ProceduralRegulation}
The summary of key issues must include:
\begin{enumerate*}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item the main relevant facts;
    \item the scope of the investigation, including the legal provisions allegedly infringed;
    \item an analysis of the views of the parties to the complaint
    \item potential corrective measures.
\end{enumerate*}\autocite[10(2)]{ProceduralRegulation}
\glspl{sac} will have four weeks -- or six, depending on the complexity of the case -- to review the summary and provide comments.\autocite[10(4)]{ProceduralRegulation}
The \gls{lsa}, within four weeks -- or six -- from the deadline for the submission of comments by \glspl{sac}, must respond to the comments and outline whether and how it plans to take them into account.\autocite[10(5)]{ProceduralRegulation}

The Procedural Regulation reiterates that all \glspl{sa} must cooperate in an endeavor to reach consensus, and that they may use all the tools provided by the cooperation procedure laid out in the \gls{gdpr} to do so.\autocite[11(1)]{ProceduralRegulation}
The Procedural Regulation introduces a new procedure to find consensus at the earlier stages of a cross-border investigation.
When a \gls{sac} disagrees with the \gls{lsa} and consensus cannot be found, the \gls{sac} can, within a month from the deadline for the \gls{lsa} to respond to the comments on the summary of key issues,\autocite[11(3)]{ProceduralRegulation} request mutual assistance or to carry out joint operations in order to reach consensus.
The \gls{lsa} must now respond to either request within one month of its receipt.\autocite[11(4)]{ProceduralRegulation}
If consensus cannot be found on the scope of the investigation and the points of law to be investigated, the \gls{lsa} must refer the matter to the \gls{edpb} requesting an urgent binding decision.\autocite[11(6)]{ProceduralRegulation}

The new complex procedure undoubtedly provides tools for the \glspl{sac} to extend the scope of an investigation in the early stages of the procedure, by focusing on information sharing through mutual assistance and joint operations. 
As seen in the previous section, much of the effectiveness of the cooperation and consistency mechanism rests on scope-setting.
The changes brought in this area do provide legal tools that could be employed to expand the breadth of facts and points of law examined, although the practical use of formal cooperation tools will be further discussed in Chapters 4 and 5.

\subsubsection{The Draft Decision}

The Procedural Regulation spells out more detailed procedures for the adoption of a final decision on a case, especially regarding the new complex procedure.
The \gls{lsa} must submit a draft decision pursuant to Article 60(3) \gls{gdpr} to the \glspl{sac} within fifteen months from $T_0$.\autocite[12(1)]{ProceduralRegulation}
The fifteen-month deadline can be extended on an exceptional basis.\autocite[12(3)]{ProceduralRegulation}
To do so, the \gls{lsa} must inform the other \glspl{sa} of the reasons and of the length of the extension.\autocite[12(3)]{ProceduralRegulation}
The \glspl{sac} may object to the extension of the deadline with a reasoned request submitted within two weeks of the receipt of the notification of the extension by the \gls{lsa}.\autocite[12(4)]{ProceduralRegulation}
However, the \gls{lsa} may still extend the deadline.\autocite[12(5)]{ProceduralRegulation}

The \glspl{sac} have a new tool at their disposal to influence enforcement in case the \gls{lsa} fails to sumbit a draft decision within the extended time limit.
\glspl{sac} may inform the \gls{lsa} that there is a need to act in order to protect the right and freedoms of data subjects.\autocite[12(5)]{ProceduralRegulation}
The \gls{sac} may adopt provisional measures on their own territory in case of a failure to deliver a decision, activating the urgency procedure in Article 66(1) \gls{gdpr}.\autocite[12(5)]{ProceduralRegulation}

\paragraph{(Partial) Dismissal of Complaints} 
When an \gls{lsa} plans to submit draft decision dismissing a complaint in whole or in part, it must first inform the receiving \gls{sa} of its preliminary view on the dismissal, before the draft decision is presented.\autocite[16(1)]{ProceduralRegulation}
The receiving \gls{sa} must then inform the complainant and provide them with an opportunity to make their views known in writing, with a time limit between three and six weeks, and must forward them to the \gls{lsa}.\autocite[16(1)--(2)]{ProceduralRegulation}
If the complainant's views do not change the preliminary assessment of the \gls{lsa}, the \gls{lsa} can proceed with the submission of the draft decision.\autocite[16(3)]{ProceduralRegulation}
This procedure forms a new formal step before the rejection of a complaint, which may change enforcement outcomes, or provide more information to the \glspl{sac} when submitting their objections to the draft decisions, as well as forming a piece of evidence for the \gls{edpb} to examine.

\subsubsection{Relevant and Reasoned Objections}

Once the \gls{lsa} submits a draft decision, the \glspl{sac} retain the right to submit relevant and reasoned objections according to Article 60(4) and 60(5) \gls{gdpr}.
The Procedural Regulation provides new rules on the content of relevant and reasoned objections, especially in the context of the new complex procedure.
A relevant and reasoned objection must be based on factual or legal elements found in the draft decision or the cooperation file, they cannot concern the scope of an investigation where consensus was reached beforehand, and they cannot concern a decision made with an early resolution procedure.\autocite[23(1)]{ProceduralRegulation}
In other words, the \glspl{sac} cannot raise objections on the scope of the investigation when it did not raise objections during the summary of key issues stage, forcing this pressure tool at the earlier stage of the procedure.

\glspl{sac} may however object to the scope of the investigation at the draft decision stage if the \gls{lsa} failed to investigate all the elements on which consensus was reached, or if new elements that pose a risk for the fundamental rights and freedoms of data subjects have surfaced since the investigation.\autocite[23(2)]{ProceduralRegulation}

The relevant and reasoned objection must be sufficiently clear, coherent and precise to enable the \glspl{sa} to prepare their position, and eventually to enable the \gls{edpb} to efficiently resolve a potential dispute.\autocite[23(3)]{ProceduralRegulation}
