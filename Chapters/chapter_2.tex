\section[The General Principle of Good Administration as a Constitutional Safeguard]{The General Principle of Good Administration\\as a Constitutional Safeguard}

General principles of \gls{eu} law represent `general, comprehensive [...], and naturally inherent'\autocite[42]{audiolux} constitutional principles that permeate the Union legal order.\autocite[7]{tridimasGeneralPrinciplesEU2020} 
They appeared as a natural consequence of the political and legal identity of the \gls{eu},\autocite[2]{TEU} a nascent legal order created by international treaties that provide a framework for incremental integration among the \glspl{ms}.\autocite[17--18]{tridimasGeneralPrinciplesBook2006}

The 1957 Treaty of Rome already empowered the Court of Justice to review legal acts,\autocite[173]{treatyofRome} but the Treaty did not provide detailed guidance on the standard of review to be applied by the court.\autocite[265--266]{craigFoundations2018} 
Therefore, in order to fill in the gaps, and in the interest of ensuring that Union institutions would face scrutiny,\autocite[269]{craigFoundations2018} the Court of Justice developed a system of background principles of law inferred from the legal and constitutional traditions of the \glspl{ms}.\autocite[269--270]{craigFoundations2018}
The work of the Court in this regard was not a mere comparative study of the principles that underpinned \glspl{ms}' legal systems, but rather an investigation of the `best' interpretation of shared values in light of the objectives of the Treaties.\autocites[270]{craigFoundations2018}[See also][point 69]{audioluxAG}

General principles of \gls{eu} law concern both principles derived from the concept of the rule of law as understood in a liberal democracy,\autocites[9]{tridimasGeneralPrinciplesEU2020}[point 69]{audioluxAG} and principles derived from the institutional design of the \gls{eu}.\autocite[11--12]{tridimasGeneralPrinciplesEU2020}
This chapter will focus on the general principle of good administration, its history, and its role as a constitutional safeguard in the European composite administrative enforcement model.

\subsection{The Concept of 'Good Administration'}

`Good administration' appears in the Union legal order both as a general principle of \gls{eu} law and as a fundamental right enshrined in the \gls{cfr}. 
This section will deal with the two sources of this right in the \gls{eu}'s legal system, and provide an analysis between the two.

\subsubsection{Good Administration as a General Principle of EU Law}

One of the first instances where the Court of Justice undertook the task of `filling in the gaps' left by the Treaties was in \emph{Algera}, one of the earliest cases of the Court.
According to the Court, while the Treaty did not contain any rules on the annulment of the administrative measures in question, it was necessary in the interest of delivering justice to infer those rules from the legal traditions of the \glspl{ms}.\autocite[para A III]{algera}

Throughout the decades, the principle of good administration has been greatly developed by the \gls{cjeu}.
The principle appears in the case law in various forms: as `good',\autocite[para 1A]{alvis} `sound',\autocite[para A II]{societesarre} `proper'\autocite[paras 7 and 12]{burban} administration. 
While today the concept of good administration is widely recognized in the jurisprudence to constitute a general principle of Union law, its early years were marked by a reluctance to recognize it as such.\autocite[66--68]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015} 

The resistance may have stemmed from the fact that the concept of good administration was referred to in inconsistent ways by the judiciary. 
For example, good administration appeared in the case law as an `obligation',\autocite[paras 245 and 279]{volkswagenAg} a `duty',\autocite[126]{bundesverbandDeutscherBanken} a `rule',\autocite[4]{vanEick} a `requirement',\autocite[71]{vba} or a `principle'.\autocite[24--31]{tetraPak} 
A narrow interpretation of the principle is also observed in cases where the Court of Justice held that while violations of the principle of good administration might have occurred, they did not have an effect on the final legality of the act in question.\autocite[67--68]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}

Nowadays, the principle of good administration is widely recognized as a general principle of Union law.\autocite[210]{hotelCipriani} 
However, the jurisprudence and the literature concede that good administration is an `umbrella' concept, without a precise delineation as a standalone principle,\autocite[points 60--61]{commVScottAG} and that it is defined by the more precise procedural rights and requirements it encompasses.\autocite[73--74]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}
The sub-principles of good administration have existed in the case law of the \gls{cjeu} independently, although the introduction of Article 41 CFR has contributed to definitively establish good administration as a general principle of law.

\subsubsection{The Charter of Fundamental Rights and Article 41 CFR}

To help discern the content of the general principle of good administration, we turn to Article 41 \gls{cfr}, which provides a number of sub-principles that can be understood as falling under the umbrella of good administration.
\autocites[75--76]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}[127]{Tillack}
The Charter of Fundamental Rights was first solemnly proclaimed in 2000 as a political document,\autocite[27--28]{fraHandbookDataprotection2018} and given binding force in 2009 with the entry into force of the Lisbon Treaty.\autocite[6(1)]{TEU}
Section V, on citizens' rights, contains Article 41, titled `Right to good administration'.\autocite[41]{CFR}
Article 41 \gls{cfr} is directly connected to the general principle of good administration established in the case law, and based on the `existence of the Union as subject to the rule of law'.\autocite[Explanation on Article 41]{ExplanationsCFR}

Article 41 \gls{cfr} provides a number of rights inserted under the umbrella of `good administration'.
As the \gls{cjeu} outlined in \emph{Tillack}:

\begin{quote}
    [The principle of sound administration] constitutes the expression of specific rights such as the right to have affairs handled impartially, fairly and within a reasonable time, the right to be heard, the right to have access to files, or the obligation to give reasons for decisions, for the purposes of Article 41 of the Charter of fundamental rights of the European Union\autocite[127]{Tillack}
\end{quote}

The wording of the Court hints at a non-exhaustive list of principles,\autocite[91--92]{hofmannEvansRelationCharterFundamental2013} similarly to Article 41(2) \gls{cfr}, which states that the right `includes' a number of specific procedural rights.\autocite[41(2)]{CFR}

One important note to make is that Article 41(1) \gls{cfr} explicitly limits the institutional scope of the Charter right to the `institutions, bodies, offices and agencies of the Union'.\autocite[41(1)]{CFR}
The Charter right also presents a narrower material scope compared to the case law principle, making references to the `individual', the right to access `his or her' file, or an `individual measure'.\autocite[86--87]{hofmannEvansRelationCharterFundamental2013}

Therefore, attention must be paid to the way these two sources of the right to good administration interact with each other.

\subsubsection[\texorpdfstring{The Coexistence of Good Administration as a General Principle\\and as a CFR Right}{The Coexistence of Good Administration as a General Principle and as a CFR Right}]{The Coexistence of Good Administration as a General Principle and as a CFR Right}

The relationship between good administration as a general principle of Union law and as a Charter right has been the subject of discussion both in the literature and in the case law.\autocite{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}

As highlighted above, the right to good administration contained in Article 41 \gls{cfr} is narrower in scope than the general principle of good administration as developed in case law.\autocite[148]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}
The \gls{cjeu} has clarified that while the right contained in the Charter does not apply to \glspl{ms},\autocite[68--69]{ysVMinister} it is a reflection of the general principle established in the case law.\autocite[49]{HNvMinisterJustice}
Good administration must be therefore observed by \glspl{ms} when acting in the scope of Union law.\autocite[50]{HNvMinisterJustice}

Looking at the \gls{gdpr}'s system of enforcement, we can affirm that the principle of good administration applies to the \glspl{dpa} when acting within the scope of Union law, and that the \gls{edpb} must respect the right to good administration in Article 41 CFR as it is a Union body.\autocite[11(1)]{EDPBRulesofProcedure}

\subsection[Good Administration in the European Composite Administrative Enforcement Model]{Good Administration in the European Composite Administrative\\Enforcement Model}

As explored earlier, the \gls{eu} presents a unique institutional design, where the Union legal order supplements the national legal orders of the \glspl{ms}. 
Consequently, \glspl{ms} are often the main enforcers of Union law, which is supposed to be applied uniformly and predictably across the territory of the Union.
In the realm of administrative enforcement, the constitutional structure of the \gls{eu} generates a `multi-level', or `composite' administrative procedure, where the administrations of different \glspl{ms} and Union institutions might be collectively responsible for carrying out enforcement on an individual case.\autocites[137--138]{hofmannCompositeDecisionMaking2009}[91--92]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}

This section will highlight the features of the `integrated' administrative model of the \gls{eu} as relates to the \gls{gdpr}'s enforcement mechanism, and the role of the principle of good administration in ensuring the rule of law across the Union legal order.

\subsubsection{The Composite Administrative Enforcement Model}

\gls{eu} law presents many policy areas where enforcement is shared among different institutional actors at the Union and national level.\autocite[138]{hofmannCompositeDecisionMaking2009}
We can distinguish between three types of cooperation: horizontal, vertical, and `diagonal'.\autocite[408]{hofmannAdministrativeLawPolicy2012}
In the context of the \gls{gdpr}, we observe a duty of horizontal cooperation between the \glspl{dpa} and a vertical relationship with the \gls{edpb}, acting as a norm-setter and dispute settlement forum.
Decisions may become even `diagonal' when their outcome is a product of the acts of different \glspl{sa} triggering the consistency mechanism, where the \gls{edpb} must examine the objections of the \glspl{sac} to reach a final decision binding on the \gls{lsa}.\autocite[267]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR}

The `composite' nature of administrative procedure in the \gls{eu} is a natural by-product of a decentralized European administration governed by subsidiarity and transjurisdictional cooperation.\autocite[91--92]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}
As Mastenbroek and Sindbjerg Martinsen argue, continuous interaction within an enforcement network may foster knowledge exchange, an increase in mutual trust, and persuasion among its members.\autocite[428]{mastenbroekFillingGapEuropean2018}
The \gls{lsa} and \glspl{sac} must reach consensus on every stage of the procedure,\autocite[465]{hofmannmustertScholtenHandbook2023} and in doing so, they must share information,\autocite[60(1)]{GDPR} provide mutual assistance to each other, and participate in joint operations.\autocite[60(2)]{GDPR}

However, issues arise from the convergence of different administrative procedural requirements established by the different legal frameworks the actors are subject to, with effects on individual cases and the overall effectiveness of judicial oversight of administrative action.\autocite[137]{hofmannCompositeDecisionMaking2009} 
Hofmann observes how the lack of systematic harmonization of administrative law exacerbates this problem in the \gls{eu}.\autocite[137]{hofmannCompositeDecisionMaking2009}

In the \gls{gdpr}'s enforcement model, decentralization was a deliberate choice of the legislator.
Council debates highlight that a proposed narrower scope of the \glspl{sac}' role was rejected, in the interest of ensuring maximum proximity to data subjects.\autocite[para 5]{councildebateSAC}
Proximity allows data subjects to lodge their complaints with their local \gls{dpa}, in a language that is familiar to them.
As a downside, it may potentially make procedures more complex, with many actors involved in the process, making intermediate administrative decisions that may be difficult to challenge.
The cross-border enforcement model of the \gls{gdpr} is heavily based on cooperation through information exchange.\autocite[140]{hofmannCompositeDecisionMaking2009}
The specific intricacies of composite administrative decision-making in the context of the \gls{gdpr} will be explored in chapters 4 and 5.

The principle of good administration however could function as a tool to fill in the gaps left by the lack of comprehensive harmonization, acting as a tool to ensure the respect of the rule of law across the complex administrative procedures taking place in the \gls{eu}.\autocites[92--93]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}[351]{nehlGoodAdminProceduralRight2009}

\subsubsection{The `Judicialization' of the Administration}

The right to good administration is explicitly linked to the right to effective judicial protection established by Article 47 \gls{cfr}.\autocites[Article 41]{ExplanationsCFR}[79--80]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}
Establishing strong procedural safeguards at the administrative stage is crucial to ensure the rule of law is upheld during the administrative procedure, and to enable an appellant to successfully challenge the actions of the administration at the judicial stage.\autocite[79--81]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}

The importance of upholding the principle of good administration as a feature of the administrative system is also augmented by the increasing complexity of the European administrative model as seen in the previous section.\autocite[86--87]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}
It would be impossible for the \gls{eu} judiciary to substitute itself to national administrative authorities and replace their power of appraisal, given the delicate institutional balance that exists between Union institutions and the \glspl{ms}.\autocite[85--87]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}

However, in a composite administrative system, national authorities may be in charge of protecting fundamental rights in the entirety of the Union's territory through the application of secondary law, as it is the case for the \gls{gdpr}. The principle of good administration becomes a crucial tool to ensure that administrations across institutional levels can be held accountable for their decisions.

\subsection{The Administration's Duty to Deliver Careful and Reasoned Decisions}

This section will explore the requirements of the sub-principles of good administration that are most relevant to the enforcement model of the \gls{gdpr} as amended by the Procedural Regulation, namely the duty of care and the duty to give reasons.

\subsubsection{The Duty of Care of the Administration}
\label{sec:DoC}

The duty of care of the administration is a key aspect of the principle of good administration.
The principle of good administration was initially developed by the \gls{cjeu} when referring to the duty of the administration to be careful in its information-gathering prior to reaching a decision.\autocite[195]{hofmannAdministrativeLawPolicy2012}
Today, the duty of care is a requirement on the administration to impartially and carefully establish the facts and points of law relevant to a case when acting in its administrative capacity to deliver a decision.\autocites[point 15]{NolleAG}[195]{hofmannAdministrativeLawPolicy2012}

The duty of care is especially relevant in cases where the administration enjoys a vast power of appraisal,\autocite[14]{TUM} acting as a check to the discretionary power by providing a minimum standard of conduct in decision-making and reason-giving.\autocite[216]{hofmannDemkovaPrinciplesProcJustice}
The administration must conduct itself in a manner that `serious',\autocite[59]{Metropole} careful, cautious,\autocite[93]{Masdar} in making its decisions.
When the administration fails to act with care, it may be found to have been `negligent', and liable for its lack of care when harm results from it.\autocite[90--91]{Masdar}

The duty of care applies to all stages of an administrative proceeding, where for example a multi-step procedure exists where a preliminary investigation may be followed up by a more in-depth one.\autocite[447]{hofmannAdministrativeLawPolicy2012} 
The administration must be careful in, for example, whether to open an investigation after a complaint was filed, with the General Court stating that `the diligent and impartial treatment of a complaint is associated with the right to sound administration which is one of the general principles that are observed in a State governed by the rule of law'.\autocite[48]{maxMobil}

According to multiple scholars, the duty of care includes the obligation of the administration to take decisions based on all relevant facts, in a way that ensures data is accurate, and following a thorough examination of the case.\autocites[412]{tridimasGeneralPrinciplesBook2006}[35]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR}[84]{hofmannEvansRelationCharterFundamental2013}
The exercise of the duty of care must lead the administration to possess the `most complete and reliable information' for making a decision.\autocite[90]{Scott}
The duty of care is therefore connected to the ability of the administration to collect, manage, and assess the information necessary to reach a decision.\autocite[36]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR} 

The scope of information-gathering, Hofmann argues, includes `all relevant aspects of a case which may have a bearing on the adoption of a measure including in cases where the decision maker has discretion.'\autocite[98]{hofmannDutyCareEU2020}
Importantly, the administration cannot simply rely on the information provided to it by the parties to the procedure, but must use its investigative powers to examine facts not initially brought by the complainant.\autocites[62]{Sytraval}[36]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR}

In synthesis, the principle of good administration serves the judiciary as a tool to assess the legality of a decision by examining the quantity and quality of the information at the disposal of the administration, and the way it was used to reach a decision.\autocite[400]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}
The duty of care requires,
\begin{enumerate*}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item at all stages of the administrative procedure,
    \item an impartial and careful establishment of the facts and legal issues, with
    \item the most complete and reliable information, through
    \item an investigation that may go beyond what the parties have provided.
\end{enumerate*}

The completeness of the information at the disposal of the administration is crucial to enable it to provide reasoned decisions, another key aspect of the principle of good administration.

\subsubsection{The Duty to Give Reasons of the Administration}

The duty to give reasons refers to the obligation for authorities to communicate the reasons motivating their decisions to the parties affected.\autocite[38]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR} 

The duty of the administration to give reasons forms part of the general principle of good administration -- especially in connection with the right to effective judicial protection.\autocites[34]{MinBZ}[57]{piVLandespolizeidirektion}
A competent authority must provide an interested party with the reasons upon which the decision is based, in order to enable them to decide whether or not to apply to the courts and for them to be in the best possible conditions to defend themselves.\autocite[63]{Penarroja}

A reasoned decision must indicate `in a concise but clear and relevant manner', the main issues of law and of fact that have led the authority to its decision, in enough detail to understand its reasoning.\autocite[page 69]{GermanyvComm}
The specific content will depend on the circumstances of each case, taking into account the content of the measure in question, the nature of the reasons provided, and the interests which the impacted parties may have in obtaining explanations.\autocite[63]{Sytraval}

As discussed by Craig, the duty to give reasons has three different functions.
First, from the viewpoint of the citizen, the right to receive reasons for a decision impacting them is important to make them understand why a decision has been adopted.\autocite[369]{craigProcess2018}
Reason-giving makes the administration transparent and accountable to the citizen, and allows them to ascertain whether the decision can be challenged in the courts.\autocite[38]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR}

Second, from the viewpoint of the judiciary, the duty of the administration to give reasons is crucial to enable judicial review of administrative acts.\autocite[370]{craigProcess2018}

Finally, from the viewpoint of the administration itself, the exercise of duty to give reasons is important as a check on the quality of the assessments that led to the decision.\autocites[38]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR}[370]{craigProcess2018}
This requirement, as argued by Opdebeek and De Somer, `forces administrative bodies to reflect on the legality, quality, rationality, reasonableness and fairness of their decisions, which may prevent bad decision-making in the first place.'\autocite[106]{opdebeekDutyGiveReasons2016}

Therefore, it follows that a diligent investigation of the facts and the points of law by the administration is necessary to ensure that the administration can provide reasoned decisions. 
In turn, the obligation to provide reasoned decisions forces the administration to be careful about the logic and the facts that contribute to their assessments, providing a check before decisions are issued.

In summary, the duty to give reasons requires the administration to provide \begin{enumerate*}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item a concise but clear and relevant indication of the main issues of law and of fact that have led to the decision,
    \item in enough detail to understand the reasoning behind the decision.
\end{enumerate*}

\subsubsection{The Duty of Care and to Give Reasons in the GDPR's Enforcement Model}

The duty to deliver careful and reasoned decisions becomes particularly important in the integrated administrative model of the \gls{gdpr}, especially when considering the objective facet of the principle of good administration.\autocite[114]{mihaescu-evansRightGoodAdministration2015}

The duty of care of \glspl{dpa} has already appeared in the case law of the \gls{cjeu}. 
The Court has ruled that \glspl{dpa} must examine complaints `with all due diligence',\autocites[63]{SchremsI}[68]{SCHUFA} meaning that they must `examine the nature of that complaint as necessary'.\autocites[109]{SchremsII}[37]{mustertCrossborderEnforcementGDPR} 
Under the framework of the \gls{gdpr}, this duty of care will only be discharged through extensive information-gathering and cooperation between the \gls{lsa} and the \glspl{sac}, so that all relevant aspects of a case can be taken into account by the \gls{lsa} when drafting a decision.

Similarly, recalling the functions of the duty to give reasons laid out by Craig,\autocite[369--370]{craigProcess2018} a close collaboration between \glspl{dpa} in a cross-border case could aid the integrated system of the \gls{gdpr} as a whole to check the quality of its assessments, ensuring that the final decision is based on lawful and well-reasoned grounds.
The integrated administrative model of the \gls{gdpr} relies heavily on the exchange of information and presupposes the ability of \glspl{dpa} to challenge each other on the quality of their legal assessments, through, \emph{inter alia}, the \glspl{rro} or the adoption of provisional measures as a derogation from the cooperation procedure.
The duty to give reasons also comes into play in the context of dispute settlement, where the \gls{edpb} needs to provide a binding decision based on the assessments and the objections of the \glspl{dpa} involved in the procedure.

However, as evidenced by Lynskey and Gentile, when an \gls{lsa} narrows the scope of the investigation and fails to collect evidence on a contested point of law, the \gls{edpb} might not have enough elements to conduct a thorough review of the case, of the quality of the draft decision of the \gls{lsa}, and of the reasoning behind the objections of the \glspl{sac}.\autocite[810]{gentileDeficientDesign2022}

The Procedural Regulation's theory to remedy the deficiencies of the \gls{gdpr}'s system of enforcement rests on a detailed proceduralization of the cooperation procedure. 
The next chapter will explore in detail the new steps introduced by the Procedural Regulation.
